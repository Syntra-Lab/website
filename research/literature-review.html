2. Introduction

The Quran presents a singular challenge and opportunity for computational linguistics. As a fixed, closed corpus of approximately 77,430 words across 6,236 verses and 114 surahs, it offers a bounded domain for rigorous annotation and analysis. Yet its language — Classical Quranic Arabic (CQA) — differs substantially from Modern Standard Arabic (MSA) and from the dialectal Arabic on which most contemporary NLP systems are trained. The morphological density of Arabic (where a single token may encode root, pattern, derivation, case, definiteness, and pronominal suffixes) amplifies these challenges significantly.

This review frames these computational techniques not as ends in themselves, but as tools in service of a rigorous, systematic methodology for Quranic interpretation. This methodology rests on three pillars:

  • Plausibility: How can computational methods be used to assess the plausibility of a given interpretation of a verse or passage by grounding it in textual evidence?
  • Verification: How can NLP techniques help verify an interpretation against all known relevant observations, including the entire Quranic text, Hadith, historical context, and linguistic norms derived from a corpus?
  • Continuous Falsifiability: An approach that supports the ongoing, iterative process of attempting to falsify interpretations. An interpretation is considered robust only if it withstands repeated attempts at falsification from new data or new analytical perspectives.

Research in this domain has accelerated since roughly 2008, driven by the release of the Quranic Arabic Corpus, the digitization of classical tafsir literature, and the emergence of transformer-based language models.

3. Foundational Challenges in Quranic Computational Linguistics

3.1. Morphological Complexity

Classical Quranic Arabic (CQA) exemplifies the challenge of non-concatenative, templatic morphology. Words are derived by interleaving a consonantal root (typically triliteral, e.g., ʿ-l-m, "to know") into a vocalic pattern that specifies grammatical and semantic information. For example, from ʿ-l-m, the Quran derives ʿalīm (All-Knowing), ʿālim (one who knows), muʿallim (teacher), and ʿilm (knowledge).

This templatic derivation is then combined with concatenative affixation. A single orthographic word in the Quran can correspond to a full clause in English. The word fasayakfīkahumu (فَسَيَكْفِيكَهُمُ, Q2:137) is a canonical example, segmenting into five morphemes: fa- (so), sa- (will), yakfī- (He will suffice), -ka (you), and -humu (them).

Formally, the generation of a surface form can be modeled as:

Word = Affixpre + Pattern(Root) + Affixsuf

3.2. Diacritics and Orthography

While modern Arabic texts typically omit short vowel diacritics, creating ambiguity, the Quran is unique in that its diacritization is canonical and part of the revealed text. The Uthmanic rasm, the script in which the Quran is written, is fully vocalized.

The primary challenge is not missing diacritics, but the existence of multiple, systematic, and rule-governed sets of readings (Qirāʾāt), such as Hafs and Warsh. These authorized recitation traditions introduce variations in vowels and sometimes consonants, which must be modeled as a systematic transformation of the base rasm, not as noise.

3.3. Semantic Ambiguity

The primary ambiguity in the Quran is not orthographic but semantic, stemming from polysemy. Many key Quranic terms have a wide semantic field that can only be disambiguated by context. For instance, the word rūḥ (روح) can refer to the angel Gabriel (Q16:102), divine inspiration or revelation (Q42:52), or the human soul/spirit (Q17:85).

The ambiguity of a word w with a set of possible senses S can be quantified by its Shannon entropy:

H(w|C) = - Σ P(si|w, C) log2 P(si|w, C)

3.4. Annotated Data Scarcity

While the Quranic text itself is a finite, closed corpus, the key bottleneck for CQA is the scarcity of large-scale, high-quality annotated data for complex downstream tasks. Resources lacking include:

  • Semantic Roles: Who did what to whom for every predicate.
  • Discourse Structure: Rhetorical relationships between clauses and verses.
  • Co-reference Resolution: Tracking all mentions of entities across the text.
  • Named Entity Normalization: Linking every entity mention to a canonical identifier.

4. Corpora and Annotated Datasets

4.1. The Quranic Arabic Corpus

The foundational resource is the Quranic Arabic Corpus (corpus.quran.com), developed by Kais Dukes at the University of Leeds. The corpus provides word-by-word morphological annotation for the entire Quran, encoding part-of-speech tags, lemmas, roots, and syntactic dependency relations. Each token is annotated with up to fourteen morphological features. The project is open-source and represents the baseline against which nearly all subsequent annotation work is measured.

A successor effort, QuranMorph (arXiv:2506.18148), revisits the morphological layer with updated annotation guidelines.

4.2. Treebank and Syntactic Datasets

MASAQ (Morphologically and Syntactically-Annotated Quran) extends morphological annotation with full syntactic parse trees. A complementary dataset adds a complete dependency annotation layer compatible with Universal Dependencies conventions.

The most architecturally ambitious treebank effort is Noor (Nashir et al., 2025, IEEE Access) — a full pipeline framework for Classical Arabic parsing that introduces the Quranic Treebank, described as "the first complete, validated, and fully machine-readable hybrid syntactic resource for Classical Arabic."

4.3. Question Answering Corpora

  • Qur'an QA 2022 — first systematic benchmark for reading comprehension in Classical Arabic.
  • HAQA and QUQA (RANLP 2023) — parallel QA corpora for both Quran and Hadith.
  • islamAndAi/QURAN-NLP — aggregates Quran text, translations, hadith, and tafaseer.

4.4. Audio and Recitation Datasets

The Quranic Audio Dataset (arXiv:2405.02675) provides aligned audio-text pairs across multiple reciters, enabling ASR, reciter identification, and tajweed violation detection.

The Tajweed Dataset (Kaggle) provides labeled examples of tajweed rules at the character and phoneme level.

5. Morphological Segmentation and Root Extraction

Morphological segmentation is the foundational task for Quranic NLP, as a single orthographic token can contain multiple morphemes including proclitics, the definite article, a stem, and pronominal enclitics.

For example, the token wa-bi-l-kitābi (وبِالْكِتَابِ, "and in the book") must be segmented into wa- (and), bi- (in), al- (the), and the stem kitāb (book).

Standard toolkits include MADAMIRA (Pasha et al., 2014) and CAMeL Tools (Obeid et al., 2020), though these require fine-tuning for CQA's unique lexicon and morphology.

6. Part-of-Speech Tagging

For Quranic Arabic, Part-of-Speech (POS) tagging is inextricably linked to morphological segmentation. The Quranic Arabic Corpus introduced a hierarchical tagset distinguishing coordinating conjunctions (CONJ), prepositions (P), definite articles (DET), verbs (V), nouns (N), and numerous subclasses.

A fully tagged token like wa-l-masjidi would be represented as morpheme-POS pairs: (wa-, CONJ) + (l-, DET) + (masjidi, N).

7. Named Entity Recognition

Named Entity Recognition (NER) in the Quran involves identifying mentions of specific entities such as persons, locations, nations, and sacred concepts. This is more challenging than in modern texts because Arabic lacks capitalization, and many proper names are derived from common word roots.

Key entity types include:

  • Prophets and Personalities: Mūsā (Moses), ʿĪsā (Jesus), Firʿawn (Pharaoh).
  • Locations: Makkah, Miṣr (Egypt), Badr.
  • Groups: Banī Isrāʾīl (Children of Israel), ʿĀd.
  • Theological Concepts: al-Tawrāt, al-Injīl, divine attributes.

State-of-the-art approaches use transformer models like CAMeLBERT-CA fine-tuned on datasets like TafsirDataset.

8. Quranic Question Answering

Question Answering (QA) over the Quranic text directly supports the pillar of Plausibility. By attempting to find direct textual answers to questions derived from an interpretation, a QA system can help assess whether the interpretation is plausible.

Research has shifted towards using LLMs for Quranic QA, using retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) frameworks where a retriever first finds relevant verses, which are then fed to the LLM as context.

9. Information Retrieval and Semantic Search

Information Retrieval (IR) and semantic search are the primary tools for the pillar of Verification. A robust search engine allows an interpreter to systematically look for confirming, contradictory, or related passages.

9.1. Alfanous

Alfanous is the most mature open-source Quranic search engine, implementing morphological normalization, root-based indexing, and transliteration handling.

9.2. Embedding-Based Retrieval

QuranGPT applies GPT-4 embeddings to all Quranic verses, enabling semantic similarity search beyond keyword matching. Vector-based approaches can uncover thematically related passages even if they do not share keywords.

10. Tafsir, NLP, and Classical Commentary

Tafsir (exegesis) represents the richest body of classical Quranic scholarship and is central to the pillars of Verification and Continuous Falsifiability. By processing this vast corpus, NLP tools enable an interpretation to be cross-verified against centuries of scholarly work.

The Tafsir API aggregates major classical tafsirs — Ibn Kathir, al-Tabari, al-Jalalayn, al-Qurtubi — in machine-readable form.

Corpus Coranicum (corpuscoranicum.de) is the primary scholarly digital humanities project for historical-critical Quranic studies.

11. Text Summarization

Arabic text summarization spans extractive (graph-based, statistical) and abstractive (neural, LLM-based) approaches. For Quranic NLP, summarization is most directly relevant to tafsir processing.

The ROUGE metric is standard for evaluation, though LEMMA-ROUGE has been proposed specifically suited to morphologically complex Arabic evaluation.

12. Machine Translation of the Quran

Machine Translation (MT) of the Quran is a task where theological and linguistic precision are paramount. Unlike translating contemporary news articles, translating the Quran involves navigating complex rhetorical structures, deep polysemy, and subtle morphological distinctions.

Modern NMT systems, especially those based on large Arabic-centric models like ALLaM (Bari et al., 2024), offer much higher fluency. However, evaluating their adequacy for Quranic translation requires human evaluation by experts.

13. Recitation, Tajweed, and Speech Processing

Tajweed constitutes a formal phonological system governing the pronunciation of every phoneme in context. The cpfair/quran-tajweed dataset provides character-level tajweed annotations.

The Quranic Audio Dataset enables ASR evaluation on recited Quranic speech, which differs from conversational Arabic in its precise diction, fixed vocabulary, and systematic phonological rules.

14. Large Language Models for Arabic and Quranic NLP

14.1. Encoder Models (BERT-family)

  • AraBERT — first Arabic BERT pre-trained on Arabic news.
  • ARBERT and MARBERT — MSA-focused and multi-dialectal BERT models.
  • CAMeLBERT — family of Arabic BERT variants pre-trained on different Arabic varieties; the Classical Arabic variant is most directly relevant to Quranic text processing.

14.2. Large Generative Arabic LLMs

  • ALLaM (Bari et al., 2024) — large Arabic-English bilingual LLM from SDAIA/NCAI Saudi Arabia.
  • Jais / Jais-chat (Sengupta et al., 2023) — Arabic-centric open generative LLM from G42 UAE.
  • AceGPT (Huang et al., 2024) — Arabic-localized LLM with cultural alignment.
  • Peacock (Alwajih et al., 2024) — Arabic multimodal LLM family.

14.3. Evaluation and Benchmarking

  • BALSAM — comprehensive Arabic LLM benchmarking platform.
  • AL-QASIDA — LLM quality in dialectal Arabic.
  • AraTrust — trustworthiness evaluation for Arabic LLMs.

14.4. Implications for Quranic NLP

Within the interpretive methodology of this review, the most promising directions are:

  1. CAMeLBERT-CLA for classical Arabic encoding tasks (morphology, POS, NER).
  2. RAG architectures for knowledge retrieval, serving the verification pillar.
  3. Interactive use of LLMs for iterative hypothesis testing and continuous falsifiability.
  4. CQA-specific pre-training using Quran, tafsir, and hadith corpora.

16. Open Problems and Research Gaps

Classical Arabic pre-training data scarcity: The Quran (~77k words) is too small to pre-train a transformer from scratch. A dedicated CQA pre-trained language model at production scale does not yet exist.

Orthographic standardization: Multiple Uthmanic rasm conventions and digitization inconsistencies mean text normalization remains an unsolved prerequisite for cross-source NLP.

Tafsir NLP: Computational tools for tafsir — semantic search, cross-tafsir comparison, entailment detection — remain largely undeveloped despite machine-readable tafsir corpora existing.

Quranic QA benchmarking for LLMs: No comprehensive benchmark exists for evaluating Arabic LLMs specifically on Quranic tasks.

Semantic and ontological modeling: Formal ontologies for Quranic concepts (RDF/OWL) remain nascent.

MSA-to-Classical transfer learning: The analogous MSA-to-Classical Arabic transfer learning problem needs systematic study.

17. Formal Framework for Computational Falsifiability

A computational interpretation I is falsifiable if and only if there exists a potential observation O such that:

Falsifiable(I) ≡ ∃O : [P(O|I) < τ] ∧ [P(O|¬I) > τ]

where τ is a falsifiability threshold (typically 0.05).

17.1. Operational Metrics

Contradiction Score (CS): Measures direct textual contradictions using semantic entailment.

Anomaly Deviation (AD): Quantifies statistical unusualness relative to corpus baselines using Kullback-Leibler divergence.

Interpretive Stability (IS): Measures robustness against minor perturbations in textual evidence.

17.2. Decision Threshold

An interpretation I is provisionally accepted if:

F(I) = (1 - CS(I)) / (1 + AD(I)) × IS(I) > θ

where θ is an acceptance threshold (empirically set to 0.7).

17.3. Implementation Workflow

  1. Formalization: Express interpretation I as a set of verifiable claims.
  2. Evidence Gathering: Retrieve supporting verses and counter-evidence.
  3. Metric Computation: Calculate CS(I), AD(I), and IS(I).
  4. Falsifiability Assessment: Compute F(I) and compare against θ.
  5. Iterative Refinement: If F(I) < θ, identify weakest claims and refine interpretation.
